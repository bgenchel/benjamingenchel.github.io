Fly With the Wind

Original Composer: McCoy Tyner
Arrangement/Transcription: Kamau Kenyatta

I display this piece here for a few reasons. I feel that among the many pieces I played as part of UC San Diego’s jazz orchestra, this piece best displays both my technique and personal style. I feel that on this piece, my personal style was adapted the best; the original recording, while having unique and impressive drumming itself, was very different from the way I interpreted it, and i’m very happy with what my personal style was able to bring. Additionally, I feel that the rest of the band also performed very well, with special kudos to the soloists.

Philosophy in a Teacup

Original Composer: Tsuneo Imahori
Arrangment/Transcription: Chase Pado

This was transcribed on my behalf by my friend and piano teacher, Chase Pado (who plays on this piece, as well as on a few of the other recordings on this page). It comes from one of my favorite shows, “Trigun”, for which Tsuneo Imahori created a really wonderful collection of pieces that combine folk, jazz, blues and electronic (specifically jungle).

Though i’ve played jazz drums for a long time, I’ve often felt not part of jazz culture, at least not as much as many of the other members of the bands i’ve played in. My personal listening time is divided among many other types of music, especially those with electronic and hip hop influences. As such, I’ve been really keen on music that could possibly translated to and performed in a jazz context; to introduce jazz players and listeners to more contemporary sounds.

I first heard philosophy in a teacup at a time when I was getting into drum and bass, jungle, and breakcore music, and so I became really enamored by the fusion between its fast intense drum breaks and delicate jazz melodies. A pronounced bass line that serves as a root or backbone is also something I’ve found I appreciate in a lot of music.

Mode to John

Original Composer: McCoy Tyner
Transcription/Arrangement: Kamau Kenyatta

Mode to John for me was a pretty pivotal point for me in my drumming. Up until this point, soloing was mostly a difficult and embarrassing part of playing for me. I like drum solos, and I really enjoy a good drum solo, especially if it has interesting structure and dynamics, but I’ve never felt that anything I did would be good enough. For whatever reason, I was able to solo fluidly on this piece, and each time with some subtle difference in practice. This song has a lot of solos, not even just one, and I didn’t hate myself every time I would play it, and that was a good feeling. I was even getting praise and support from the rest of the band. When it came to the actual performance, I put a lot of pressure on myself to play this piece in the most impressive way possible, and as a result I think I did not play this piece with as much thought or fluidity as I had played it in rehearsal. When I listen to the recording now, I feel I favored more blustering, loud, fast fills over subtlety. Overall, I still liked the way it came out, and proud of my accomplishment in playing it.

Additionally, speed swing was very difficult for me for a long time, and though I did not have much time to practice drums in college due to my engineering work, I was able to practice consistent swung triplets whenever I had time, just patting my lap. I think that shows through on this piece. Though the tempo is relatively high, I can hear that I was much more able to keep up the speed without sacrificing the full swing rhythm on my right hand. In general, I like the way I was able to incorporate various ghost notes and fills, as well as follow the soloist.

Oliloque Valley

Original Composer: Herbie Hancock
Transcription/Arrangment: Kamau Kenyatta

I have many recordings from the 5 years I spent in UCSD’s jazz group, but this piece stood out to me because of how clean it sounded relative to the others, both in terms of my playing and that of the other members. I also enjoy that it moves between multiple styles. Most of the other pieces on this page are heavy on speed, and density in terms of my playing, but this piece has me playing much quieter, and with more subtlety. I’m happy with my performance here in a different way than I am with my playing on the other pieces displayed here. For the other pieces, I chose those which I felt framed my drumming the best (since this is my portfolio) but I feel that this recording of Oliloque Valley shows how my drumming frames the piece as a whole best; how it blends in and works with the other players, which is equal to if not exceeding style in importance.

Animals

Original Composer: Bonobo (Simon Green)
Transcription/Arrangement: Benjamin Genchel

I’m very proud of this recording because it was such an effort to transcribe the piece and direct the band. As a drummer, I didn’t need to read music (only rhythm marks), or know any theory, so everything I knew how to play on melodic instruments was learned through tabs, by watching others play, or by ear. Without that knowledge, it’s very difficult to transcribe and lead. However, since playing in jazz band in high school, I had wanted to take more ownership of my musicianship by finding jazz style music that I liked and introducing it to the band and playing it.

I was introduced to Bonobo’s black sands album in 2011, and, “Animals” really stood out to me. While the experience of really enjoying a piece of music, or really art in general can be expressed with beautiful metaphor, I think what I liked about it was the rhythm the on the drums (I would characterize it as, fast and funky with a lot of ghost notes, exactly my style), the ⅞ time signature, and the bold, repeating bassline. All those elements made the piece really interesting to me; additionally, I found out later that the majority of the music was sampled and mixed electronically, giving it a modern appeal.

I would say that I started to transcribe this piece in the fall of 2011, and it took all the way up until June of 2012 to actually get it in a state ready to play. I started by listening to the song over and over again until I could get some of the chords out, but the chords I had heard were either really simplistic, or mismatched in key. Kamau Kenyatta, the band’s director, was able to help me figure out the real chords in a quick listening session. After that was set, transcribing all the parts and melodies into Sebelius took about 4 months with my engineering classes and research schedule.

Because I was unfamiliar with transcribing, I used Sebelius’s midi playback feature as a measure of correctness. As a result, the first version of the chart used only 8th notes and relied entirely on ties and rests to define note length.

Getting this done and performed was a big step in my confidence as a musician, and the first time that my melodic skills were applied in a professional context. Unfortunately, it would be the last for a while as my engineering curriculum and projects began to take up more of my time, and I no longer had room in my schedule to apply my below average transcribing skills over a long period of time. I had hoped to follow this piece with “Philosophy in a Teacup,” but I ended up waiting until I could find another person to help me with the transcription.

Production/Composition

Second

This is a work in progress piece written and produced by me with help and guidance from my teacher, Cody Farwell.

I think this piece reflects heavily my desire to introduce chaos and noise into my music. Though I'm not always in the mood, I really appreciate music that attempts novelty, goes off the beaten path, or suprises me. In that sense, I almost feel that breaking down a song's melody and rhythm, introducing stabbing, or startling noises that are disharmonious or arhythmic, is almost the easy way of doing that. In that spirit, the intro jumps around across several octaves, drops in and out, trading with the percussion, and doesn't follow a particular meter. It then drops into a lead melody that contains 3 bars of 4, giving an almost odd meter feel.

Like most of the music that I make, this started out with just playing around with synths until I found a sound I liked. What I came up with, which is part of what made it to the track, was a gliding, modulated square and sine wave that sounded a bit like R2D2 when moving from high to low. The robotic sounding lead led me to attempt a piece in the style of Tomonojo Miyagi, a japanese artist that similarly incorporates a lot of static, distortion and machine noise samples into his ambient styled works.

What came about between sessions with my teacher strayed from that path a bit, though I'm not unhappy for how it's turning out.

First

This first song I worked on with my production teacher, Cody Farwell. As a result of being the first, it's served as a playground for attempting and demonstrating production techniques with composition applied as an afterthought between lessons.

Even now with how far it's come, the result of it really being a sort of trial track shows in it's overall structure, which is that it doesn't really have much structure. It moves from section to section, keeping similar musical themes, but without too much regard for a particular direction or feel, though not for lack of effort.

The first section was made entirely by me, which I think shows in it's style, which is much more akin to my tastes. It's smooth and ambient, with wide chords and a bit odd in terms of rhythm. What comes after is a result of different experiments that lead it to a more mainstream electronic sound, with a softer build up leading to a big, danceable drop.

Suprisingly, my favorite section ended up being the bridge following the drop, with it's syncopated purcussion and funky bassline. For a while, that section served as a sort of graveyard for concepts that didn't really fit in to the piece. I feel now that that added more character.

Beginning

A short piece I wrote and produced as part of a beat Cypher for UC San Diego's Producers and Remixers Organization.

I don't have much to say on this piece in terms of structure and concept; really it came about naturally, just from playing around with a few samples.

By far my favorite part is the lead, which is actually just a flute sample that's had a few effects added to it, played as a keyboard. I was taking jazz piano lessons at the time, and I was really eager to try to apply what I had learned in those lessons to production. As such, the lead is just that, a series of jazzy chords. Actually, I first came up with the ending progression, then came up with the main progression to accompany the background synth. I'm really happy with how both came out; they contain so much feeling (at least to me). The main progression seems like a call to battle, with what follows sounding like a peaceful reprieve. That the sound is derived from a wind instrument certainly lends to that aesthetic; I've often imagined valkeries in the sky singing and blowing their horns over a war waged on the ground when listening to this piece.

Organized Sound

This piece was composed as a final project for my Electronic Music History class at UC San Diego, from the derivative concept of a sound collage or timeline for the history of electronic music, at least up until the 1960s. It takes it's title from Edgar Varese's term for describing his own musical style. Each section represents a different era in electronic music history, beginning with noise instruments and concluding with a reproduction of 'Bicycle Built for Two,', an homage to Max Matthew's work at Bell Labs in 1962.

When taking this class, I had to make a large leaps in terms of my artistic appreciation in order to tolerate all of the crazy pieces we studied. It was difficult but I do feel in retrospect that it opened me to greater meaning in art, and especially in music. Up until that point, the most abstract music I had listened to was freeform jazz, which was really more of a joke to me than a form of music.

Following suit from the course material, I tried to include a lot of chaos, tension, randomness, and disharmony. Friends who were shown this piece later mostly described it as crazy and scary. But perhaps to ascribe those qualities to the source was only my interpretation, and the true meaning in that material was lost on me. I suppose in that case this piece can at the very least serve as a lens through which to view abstract art in the way that a student who doesn't understand sees it.

The majority of the piece is comprised of samples, with only the 'electronic sounds' portion using synths created in ableton, though still interspersed with samples from one of Karlheinz Stockhausen's works.

This piece received acclaim from both my professor and TA's, with each emailing me after the presentation to congratulate me on it.

This is the first piece I ever produced using Ableton Live, and really what I would consider my first production, since my prior attempts were haphazardly thrown together using demo software. As a result, this is the first piece where I didn't just write notes down to be played in a sequencer, but designed and controlled sounds and effects at a somewhat fine grain.

This piece was written/produced for an assignment given in my electronic music history class. The assignment was very simple, just record sounds and pitch them up and down. I however, decided to showboat and so made a passion project out of it.

In line with the assignment, all the sounds sans voice samples (taken from the audio for Goku's super saiyan 3 transformation from Akira Torriyama's DragonBall Z) were sounds recorded from around my dorm using the microphone on my years old hand-me-down HP laptop.

The melody and key for the piece were inspired and provided respectively by the fundemental frequencies and accompanying harmonics from a recording of a giant fan being used to mitigate the unpleasant scents coming from UCSD's sewers; a true 'look on the bright side' situation. Other samples used include me cracking my knuckles, slamming a desk drawer and, hitting a pot with a wooden spoon.

CleverPet

2016 promotional video showcasing the CleverPet Hub

CleverPet is a San Diego based startup that produces an IOT training console for pets. The founders, computational neuroscience PhDs from UCSD that specialized in training animals with computers, sought to bring their research to the consumer market to benefit animals and owners alike. CleverPet seeks to both enrich the lives of pets through automated engagement as well as reveal their cognitive potential to their owners.

Me and Kara Knudsen, Head of Customer Experience, presenting the CleverPet Hub at CES 2016

It was my absolute pleasure to work as an engineer for CleverPet through an internship in the summer of 2015 and year working as a full time software engineer. My primary work and responsibilty was ownership of the backend. I architected and built the current running version of the backend API in Python on the Google Cloud Platform, and continued to maintain and expand it over time. Designing the backend included modeling data for the client ecosystem (pets, owners, devices), exposing routes for sending and recieving formatted data, processing, and authentication. In addition, I wrote and rewrote large sets of unit tests, implemented parallelized load testing using Google Compute instances, and provided several utility scripts for the rest of the team to interface with the API and datastore.

I also developed and debugged firmware running on the device's microntroller: The Particle Photon. I wrote code specifically for interfacing with the cloud backend; modules that requested, parsed and confirmed the receipt of new games, as well as modules that managed the send and receive of messages over tcp.

The Particle Photon; used as a development board for the CleverPet Hub.

Because CleverPet was so small during my time there, I often worked on miscellaneous tasks whenever they popped up and jumped around from project to project. I developed a promotional site for a conference, tested hardware, assembled demos, and debugged and contributed code for the mobile app to name a few.

Intellisis

Intellisis, now known as KnuEdge , is a technology start up based in San Diego founded and headed by former NASA head of administration Dan Goldin, with the general purpose of making innovations in hardware and software systems based on the functions of biological systems. KnuEdge now acts as an umbrella corporation over a few other more specifically directed companies all of which used to be Intellisis. While at Intellisis during the summer of 2014, I worked as a Software/Signal Processing Intern in the group that seems to have become the spin off company KnuVerse.

The logo for KnuVerse, a subsidiary of KnuEdge. KnuVerse encapsulates the work I did while at Intellisis.

My team worked on developing a military grade voice recognition based security system called the AudioPIN, and building it into various client side applications such as a web app, VoIP, and mobile app (Android) to demonstrate its wide ranging applicability. During my internship, I primarily worked on three projects:

  • Parallelizing python 'nose' unit tests for the voice recognition system using Oracle's Sun Grid Engine.
  • Building a front end desktop application using PyQT for testing and gathering data on 'over the shoulder' attacks.
  • Developing an Android App for the purpose of demonstrating the mobile potential of the system.

My primary project, and certainly the one that took the most time and effort was building the android app. The project was initially going to be passed on to a contractor, but I volunteered to help out because I had taught myself some android the previous summer and was interested in learning more. Eventually, I became wrapped up in the work, and was making progress quickly enough for the team to forgo the contractor and allow me to develop the project independently. I finished a functioning app; it allowed a user to train the system on their voice, choose various settings, and set a rudimentary lock screen using the system.

Though my work particularly was undocumented (this project was under wraps during my time there), a later iteration of my project is demonstrated in the video below.

Theta Tau Garnett

The very simple logo for Garnett, designed in Canva.

As the webmaster for my professional engineering fraternity, I proposed and led a team to develop an internal organizational webapp in order to facilitate task completion and better organization and flow of information through the active body.

The idea sprouted from the organization's inefficient use of multiple technologies; when I began my tenure, information was evenly distributed between a chaotic private forum, a facebook group, various mobile chat apps and google docs. Nothing appeared coherent, and members were constantly uniformed in various ways though one could easily envision a space in which all the needs those technologies filled were satisfied and integrated in a coherent manner.

Over two years, the project evolved in concept in technology. When I began leading the project, my teamates and I (to a much greater extent) had minimal experience with web development; I had hoped we could all learn through working on it. What that meant though, was that there was very little specific direction for progress. I had thought that Php was still the language of the web, and codecademy's course on the language further convinced me of that notion. Further, I personally had little experience leading, and no experience leading a software team. By the end of the first year, we had some good ideas out, a few pivots, and a function login screen.

Fortunately, at the end of the year, I was able to recruit the help of a few senior members that acted as co leaders and helped to push the project forward. We started from scratch with modern tools: Node.js, Angular.js, Heroku, and PosgreSQL. We had conceptual meetings and came up with a more concrete direction for the app - a timeline centric social network built around a core 'event' data model that could be generalized to a wide range of conceptual entities. In addition, we cleaned out inactive members and brought on younger members that we could mentor and who could continue the project after we had graduated. Mentorship, and the creation of a base that could be used as a project and teaching tool to boost the careers of younger members became a central goal of the project.

As a team lead, I organized weekly meetings, attempted to implement agile coding practices and worked to divide tasks in a way that would give people room to learn and work in areas they were interested in. I worked closely with my co-leaders to choose appropriate technologies for development and deployment, implement proper coding practices, and drive development forward both in terms of defining direction and contributing code. In addition, I served as designer and created mockups (seen here) to define the look and function of various app componenets.

PureData Drum Machine

A demonstration of the main patch as used for generating rough, random beats.

This PureData patch was developed as a final project for my Computer Music class at UC San Diego. As a drummer, I was interested in attempting to synthesize drum sounds from scratch. Additionally, through the course and through my attempts to produce music in my free time, I came to realize how inspirational randomness was for me. I attempted to combine these ideas in this patch, a drum machine with accompanying random bassline.

I think this patch is cool because on its own it's able to produce something akin to listenable music, and some of the basslines that come out of just random assignment are mesmerizing. Additionally, because the line is purely random, it forces me to digest and build on music that doesn't adhere to a particular timing or key.

The snare, kick and high hat sound standard; they kind of remind me of 8 bit instruments. The crash was the hardest to work on and also the instrument that sounds least like it "should". The had read a small blurb on the internet saying that a crash sound was made by having many frequencies decay at different rates, with the high frequencies lasting much longer than the low frequencies. In retrospect, I should have included many more frequencies, covering the frequency space with much higher resolution. Though what I got was certainly not what I was looking for, I still found the sound interesting enough to keep. To me, it sounds sort of like an electronic version of hitting the inside of a piano with a long paddle.

bhglib/practice

In my little bits of spare time, I often find myself working on practice questions for interviews or tutorials for things I find interesting. I realized after some time that in working on these types of things, I was generating a lot of potentially useful code (as well as sample code to show to those who request it), and since I almost singularly in Python I thought it could be useful to compile those snippets into a library that I could import for future projects. The 'bhglib' repo was started with that intent. It is small now, and doesn't have to much in it, but my hope is that it will keep growing as I keep coding both in personal projects and academia/industry.

In addition, I also have many many practice problems that I save that aren't particularly generalizable (or at least, I haven't seen how to generalize them yet) and so I've also began posting those as a repo separate from the libary. I feel that it's good to keep a record of all work done, as well as share solutions publicly for those who might find them useful.
